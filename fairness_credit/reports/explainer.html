Fairness in Machine Learning:
Theory & Practice

A graduate-level walkthrough of what our code is actually doing — from the probability model underlying logistic regression to the impossibility theorem that sits at the heart of every fairness debate.

Introduction

We built a classifier that predicts whether an individual earns more than $50,000 per year, then measured how fairly it treats people of different racial groups. That one-sentence description conceals a surprisingly rich set of mathematical questions. What does it mean to predict something? How does a boosted tree differ from a logistic model — not just in performance but in its mathematical structure? And most importantly: what does it mean for a classifier to be fair, and can we always achieve fairness?

This document answers those questions carefully, building from first principles. You already know regression; the goal here is to show you how everything connects to deeper statistical ideas you may have seen in other contexts.

1. The Data

1.1 The American Community Survey

Every year, the U.S. Census Bureau randomly samples roughly 3.5 million households through the American Community Survey (ACS). Unlike the decennial census (which counts everyone), the ACS is a probability sample — each housing unit has a known, non-zero probability of selection, and respondents are legally required to respond.

From a statistical standpoint, the ACS is a stratified cluster sample: the country is divided into Primary Sampling Units (PSUs), which are sampled with probability proportional to size, and then addresses within PSUs are sampled. Each record comes with a survey weight PWGTP — the number of people in the population that this respondent represents. For a proper population-level analysis you should use these weights; for our purposes (training a classifier) we treat the sample as roughly iid, which is a simplification worth knowing you are making.

The 2018 California 1-Year file contains 195,665 individual records. California is the largest state sample and chosen because its demographic diversity makes the fairness analysis more interesting.

1.2 Folktables: Standardizing ACS for ML Research

The raw ACS data requires significant preprocessing. The Folktables library (Ding et al., 2021, NeurIPS) packages a set of canonical prediction tasks derived from ACS, designed specifically for fairness research. The key contribution is a principled answer to a question that sounds trivial but isn't: which columns should you use, and how should you handle missingness?

The ACSIncome task applies the following filter before giving you data:

\[ \text{Include individual } i \text{ if: } \text{AGEP}_i \geq 16 \;\wedge\; \text{PINCP}_i > 100 \;\wedge\; \text{WKHP}_i > 0 \;\wedge\; \text{PWGTP}_i \geq 1 \]

Age at least 16, positive income above $100 (filters out negative income from business losses), working at least 1 hour per week. This makes the prediction task meaningful — we're not predicting income for retirees or children.

The label is a binary threshold: does annual personal income exceed $50,000? This threshold is arbitrary — it happens to be roughly the 2018 U.S. median individual income. A different threshold would give different base rates and different fairness numbers, which is itself an important sensitivity to be aware of.

This is called proxy discrimination, and it means removing a sensitive attribute from the feature set does not, by itself, produce a fair model.

2. Logistic Regression

2.1 The Probability Model

Logistic regression is often introduced as "regression for classification," but that framing undersells it. It is more precisely a generalized linear model (GLM) for a Bernoulli-distributed outcome. Understanding it from this angle makes the loss function obvious rather than mysterious.

Let \(Y_i \in \{0, 1\}\) be the income label for individual \(i\), and let \(\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{10}\) be their feature vector. We model:

\[ Y_i \;\sim\; \text{Bernoulli}(p_i), \qquad p_i = P(Y_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i) \]

The challenge is that \(p_i\) must lie in \([0,1]\), but a linear predictor \(\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i\) can take any value in \(\mathbb{R}\). We need a link function to map between them. The logistic model uses the logit link:

\[ \log \frac{p_i}{1 - p_i} = \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i \]

Solving for \(p_i\) gives the sigmoid function:

\[ p_i = \sigma(\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i}} \]

The logit \(\log[p/(1-p)]\) is the log-odds of the outcome. A coefficient \(\beta_j\) represents the change in log-odds for a one-unit increase in \(x_j\), holding everything else constant. This has a direct interpretation: \(e^{\beta_j}\) is the multiplicative change in the odds ratio.

🔍 Intuition: Why the logit?

The logit is the canonical link function for the Bernoulli family in exponential family theory — it arises naturally, not by arbitrary choice. More concretely: if you think of the log-odds as a "latent score" of creditworthiness or income potential, the logistic model says that score is a linear function of the features. The sigmoid then converts that score to a probability.

2.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation and Cross-Entropy Loss

We estimate \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) by maximum likelihood. The likelihood of observing the data is:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^n p_i^{y_i} (1 - p_i)^{1 - y_i} \]

Taking the negative log (for minimization) gives the binary cross-entropy loss:

\[ \ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left[ y_i \log p_i + (1 - y_i) \log(1 - p_i) \right] \]

This loss has a beautiful information-theoretic interpretation: it is the KL divergence between the empirical distribution of labels and the model's predicted distribution, averaged over samples. Minimizing it is equivalent to finding the distribution closest (in KL sense) to the true data-generating process, within the family of logistic models.

2.3 Optimization and Regularization

Unlike ordinary least squares, logistic regression has no closed-form solution. sklearn's LogisticRegression uses a coordinate-descent algorithm (for L1) or L-BFGS (for L2 or no regularization). By default it applies L2 regularization (ridge), adding \(\frac{1}{2C} \|\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2\) to the loss. With max_iter=1000 and StandardScaler, the features are standardized so that all coefficients are on comparable scales and the optimization landscape is well-conditioned.

We call StandardScaler because gradient-based optimization is sensitive to feature scale — if one feature ranges over [0, 500] and another over [0, 2], the gradient landscape is poorly scaled and convergence is slow. After scaling, each feature has mean 0 and variance 1.

3. XGBoost: Gradient Boosted Trees

3.1 The Core Idea of Boosting

Boosting is a meta-algorithm for turning a collection of weak learners into a strong one. The key insight, due to Schapire (1990), is that a learner only needs to be slightly better than random guessing (a "weak learner") to be useful — you can boost it into an arbitrarily accurate predictor.

The intuition: train a model on the data, look at where it made mistakes, upweight those examples, train another model, and repeat. Each new model focuses on the residual errors of its predecessors. The final prediction is a weighted vote of all models.

Gradient boosting (Friedman 2001) makes this precise using the language of optimization. We are fitting an additive model:

\[ F_T(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} f_t(\mathbf{x}) \]

where each \(f_t\) is a decision tree, and we want to minimize some loss \(\sum_i \ell(y_i, F_T(\mathbf{x}_i))\). We cannot optimize over all trees simultaneously — the space is too large. Instead, we build the model greedily: given the current ensemble \(F_{t-1}\), find the \(f_t\) that most reduces the loss.

3.2 The XGBoost Objective: Second-Order Taylor Expansion

The XGBoost contribution (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) is to approximate the loss using a second-order Taylor expansion. At step \(t\), the current predictions are \(\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}\). We want to add a new tree \(f_t\) such that:

\[ \mathcal{L}^{(t)} = \sum_{i=1}^n \ell\!\left(y_i,\; \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)\right) + \Omega(f_t) \]

Taylor-expanding to second order in \(f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)\) around \(\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}\):

\[ \mathcal{L}^{(t)} \approx \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ \ell(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}) + g_i\, f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) + \tfrac{1}{2} h_i\, f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)^2 \right] + \Omega(f_t) \]

where we define the first and second derivatives of the loss with respect to the current prediction:

\[ g_i = \frac{\partial \,\ell(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})}{\partial\, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}} \qquad h_i = \frac{\partial^2 \ell(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})}{\partial (\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})^2} \]
🔍 Why second order?

The first derivative \(g_i\) is the gradient — it tells you which direction to move. The second derivative \(h_i\) is the curvature — it tells you how large a step to take. Using both is analogous to Newton's method vs. gradient descent. Vanilla gradient boosting only uses \(g_i\); XGBoost uses both, which makes the step sizes more intelligent and convergence faster. For the binary cross-entropy loss, \(g_i = \hat{p}_i - y_i\) (prediction error) and \(h_i = \hat{p}_i(1 - \hat{p}_i)\) (variance of the prediction).

Dropping the constant term \(\ell(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})\), the objective we're minimizing over the structure of \(f_t\) is:

\[ \tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} = \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ g_i f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) + \tfrac{1}{2} h_i f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)^2 \right] + \Omega(f_t) \]

3.3 Optimal Leaf Weights

A tree \(f_t\) partitions the feature space into \(J\) leaves. Let \(I_j = \{i : \mathbf{x}_i \in \text{leaf } j\}\) be the set of training examples falling in leaf \(j\), and let \(w_j \in \mathbb{R}\) be the output value of leaf \(j\). The regularization term is:

\[ \Omega(f_t) = \gamma J + \frac{1}{2}\lambda \sum_{j=1}^J w_j^2 \]

where \(\gamma\) penalizes the number of leaves (complexity) and \(\lambda\) is an L2 penalty on the leaf weights. Substituting into the objective and grouping by leaf:

\[ \tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} = \sum_{j=1}^J \left[ \left(\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i\right) w_j + \frac{1}{2}\left(\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda\right) w_j^2 \right] + \gamma J \]

This is a sum of independent quadratics in each \(w_j\). Each quadratic \(G_j w + \frac{1}{2}(H_j + \lambda)w^2\) is minimized by completing the square, giving the optimal leaf weight:

\[ \boxed{w_j^* = -\frac{G_j}{H_j + \lambda}, \quad G_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} g_i, \quad H_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} h_i} \]

The corresponding minimum value of the objective for a given tree structure is:

\[ \tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)*} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^J \frac{G_j^2}{H_j + \lambda} + \gamma J \]

This score is used to evaluate candidate splits. A split of leaf \(j\) into children \(L\) and \(R\) is accepted if the gain is positive:

\[ \text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2}\left[ \frac{G_L^2}{H_L + \lambda} + \frac{G_R^2}{H_R + \lambda} - \frac{(G_L+G_R)^2}{H_L+H_R+\lambda} \right] - \gamma \]

4. Fairness Metrics

We have a binary classifier \(\hat{Y} = \mathbf{1}[f(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0.5]\), a true label \(Y \in \{0, 1\}\), and a protected attribute \(A \in \{0, 1\}\) (here: race, simplified to White vs. Black for the main comparison). The question is: in what sense should \(\hat{Y}\) treat groups \(A=0\) and \(A=1\) equally?

This turns out to be a genuinely hard question. There are many mathematically distinct definitions of fairness, and as we will see, they are often mutually incompatible. The three definitions below are the most widely used in the academic and regulatory literature.

4.1 Demographic Parity (Statistical Parity)

Definition

A classifier satisfies demographic parity with respect to protected attribute \(A\) if: \[ P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 1) \] The demographic parity difference is \(\Delta_{DP} = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=1) - P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=0)\).

This is the most intuitive definition: the model should approve people at the same rate across groups. It is what most people mean when they say "the model treats everyone equally."

The problem: demographic parity is blind to whether the groups actually have different outcome rates. If Group A has a true income-over-50k rate of 44% and Group B has a rate of 34%, then a perfect classifier — one that never makes mistakes — will violate demographic parity. Requiring parity forces the model to either approve unqualified Group B members or deny qualified Group A members. This is not obviously fair.

In our data: White base rate = 44.3%, Black base rate = 34.5%. The gap is 9.8 percentage points, and it exists in the ground truth, before any model is applied. This reflects real economic inequality, which the model cannot fix.

4.2 Equalized Odds

Definition (Hardt, Price & Srebro, 2016)

A classifier satisfies equalized odds if, for both \(y \in \{0, 1\}\): \[ P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = y, A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = y, A = 1) \] This requires equal true positive rates (TPR) and equal false positive rates (FPR) across groups.

Equalized odds conditions on the true label \(Y\). This is a crucial distinction: rather than asking "do groups get approved at the same rate?" it asks "given that you actually earn over $50k, do you have an equal chance of the model recognizing that, regardless of your race?"

The TPR gap measures whether the model equally identifies high-income individuals across groups. In our XGBoost results: TPR(White) = 0.808, TPR(Black) = 0.686. This means that among people who actually earn over $50k, Black individuals are significantly more likely to be misclassified as low-income. If this were a credit model, they'd be denied a loan they deserve.

The FPR gap measures whether the model equally approves low-income individuals across groups. Here the gap is small (0.025), meaning the model isn't particularly more likely to falsely approve White applicants than Black applicants.

The equalized odds difference in our code is defined as:

\[ \Delta_{EO} = \max\!\Big(|\text{TPR}_0 - \text{TPR}_1|,\; |\text{FPR}_0 - \text{FPR}_1|\Big) \]

taking the max because you must satisfy both conditions simultaneously.

4.3 Calibration

Definition

A score function \(s(\mathbf{x}) \in [0,1]\) is calibrated within groups if for all \(p \in [0,1]\) and for all \(a \in \{0,1\}\): \[ P\!\left(Y = 1 \mid s(\mathbf{x}) = p,\, A = a\right) = p \] The score means the same thing regardless of which group you belong to.

Calibration is about the meaning of the score, not the decisions made from it. If a model assigns a score of 0.7 to an applicant, calibration requires that applicant to have a 70% chance of earning over $50k — whether they are White or Black.

In our code, we approximate calibration by bucketing scores into 5 equal-width bins and measuring the average actual outcome rate within each bin, separately by race. The calibration difference is the average absolute difference across bins.

Notice our results: XGBoost has a calibration difference of 0.005 (near-perfect), while logistic regression has 0.022. XGBoost produces more meaningful scores. But it has worse equalized odds. This is the impossibility theorem showing up in our data, and we explore it next.

5. The Impossibility Theorem

5.1 The Chouldechova Theorem

Theorem (Chouldechova, 2017)

Let \(\text{PPV}_a = P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=1, A=a)\) (positive predictive value / precision) and \(\text{FOR}_a = P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=0, A=a)\) (false omission rate). If the base rates differ, i.e. \(P(Y=1 \mid A=0) \neq P(Y=1 \mid A=1)\), then a classifier cannot simultaneously satisfy:

  1. Equal false positive rates: \(\text{FPR}_0 = \text{FPR}_1\)
  2. Equal false negative rates (i.e., equal TPR): \(\text{FNR}_0 = \text{FNR}_1\)
  3. Equal positive predictive values: \(\text{PPV}_0 = \text{PPV}_1\)
unless it achieves perfect accuracy.

5.2 Derivation from Bayes' Theorem

The proof is elegant and short. It follows from applying Bayes' theorem to express PPV in terms of FPR, FNR, and the base rate.

Let \(\pi_a = P(Y=1 \mid A=a)\) be the base rate in group \(a\). Let \(\text{FPR}_a = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0, A=a)\) and \(\text{FNR}_a = P(\hat{Y}=0 \mid Y=1, A=a) = 1 - \text{TPR}_a\). The PPV (precision) in group \(a\) is:

\[ \text{PPV}_a = P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=1, A=a) = \frac{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1, A=a)\, P(Y=1 \mid A=a)}{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=a)} \]

The denominator \(P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=a)\) expands by the law of total probability:

\[ P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=a) = \text{TPR}_a \cdot \pi_a + \text{FPR}_a \cdot (1 - \pi_a) \]

Substituting \(\text{TPR}_a = 1 - \text{FNR}_a\):

\[ \text{PPV}_a = \frac{(1 - \text{FNR}_a)\,\pi_a}{(1 - \text{FNR}_a)\,\pi_a + \text{FPR}_a\,(1 - \pi_a)} \]
Proof of Impossibility

Suppose the classifier achieves equal FPR (\(\text{FPR}_0 = \text{FPR}_1 =: p\)) and equal FNR (\(\text{FNR}_0 = \text{FNR}_1 =: q\)). Then for group \(a\):

\[ \text{PPV}_a = \frac{(1-q)\,\pi_a}{(1-q)\,\pi_a + p\,(1-\pi_a)} \]

For \(\text{PPV}_0 = \text{PPV}_1\) we need:

\[ \frac{(1-q)\,\pi_0}{(1-q)\,\pi_0 + p\,(1-\pi_0)} = \frac{(1-q)\,\pi_1}{(1-q)\,\pi_1 + p\,(1-\pi_1)} \]

Cross-multiplying and canceling \((1-q)\):

\[ \pi_0\Big[(1-q)\,\pi_1 + p(1-\pi_1)\Big] = \pi_1\Big[(1-q)\,\pi_0 + p(1-\pi_0)\Big] \] \[ \pi_0\, p(1-\pi_1) = \pi_1\, p(1-\pi_0) \] \[ p\,(\pi_0 - \pi_1) = 0 \]

This holds if and only if \(p = 0\) (perfect FPR, i.e. no false positives) or \(\pi_0 = \pi_1\) (equal base rates). Since we assumed base rates differ, equal PPV requires \(p = 0\). By a symmetric argument, \(q = 0\) as well. But \(p = q = 0\) means the classifier makes no errors — perfect accuracy.

□
⚠ What this means practically

In our data, White base rate = 44.3%, Black base rate = 34.5%. The gap is real. Therefore, any classifier (no matter how good) that correctly identifies high-income individuals at equal rates across groups (equalized odds) must assign different predictive meanings to the same score across groups (violating calibration / PPV parity). And vice versa. There is no free lunch. Policy choices about which fairness criterion to prioritize are unavoidable — and they are fundamentally value judgments, not technical ones.

The COMPAS Controversy

This theorem is not abstract. In 2016, ProPublica published an analysis of COMPAS, a recidivism-prediction algorithm used in U.S. courts for bail and sentencing decisions. They found that Black defendants were roughly twice as likely as White defendants to be falsely flagged as high-risk (FPR disparity). Northpointe, the company behind COMPAS, responded that the algorithm was calibrated — a score of 7 meant the same recidivism probability for Black and White defendants. Both were right. The Chouldechova theorem guarantees that given different base rates (Black defendants do have higher observed recidivism rates in the data, themselves a product of systemic discrimination in policing and prosecution), you cannot have both.

The AUC Gap

The AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) measures the probability that the model ranks a random positive example above a random negative example. It is threshold-free — it doesn't depend on where we set the 0.5 cutoff. XGBoost achieves 0.907 vs. logistic regression's 0.861. The gap of 4.6 percentage points represents a meaningful improvement. In credit decision contexts, differences of even 1–2 AUC points translate to significant financial impact.

Why XGBoost Has Worse Fairness Gaps

XGBoost is a more powerful model — it captures nonlinear interactions that logistic regression cannot. This means it "finds" more signal in the data, including signal that is correlated with race. Recall that OCCP (occupation) and POBP (place of birth) are both highly predictive of income and correlated with race due to historical occupational segregation and immigration patterns. A more powerful model extracts more of this correlated signal, producing sharper predictions that are also more racially disparate.

The Calibration Inversion

Logistic regression has worse calibration (0.022) but better equalized odds (0.087 vs. 0.122). This is precisely the impossibility theorem in action. Logistic regression's relative inflexibility means it cannot fully separate the groups, so it produces more equalized error rates — but at the cost of being less well-calibrated. XGBoost's scores are highly meaningful (calibration diff = 0.005), but meaningful scores that accurately reflect real economic inequality produce large approval gaps.

A Note on What's Causing the Gap

The critical question for any fairness intervention is: how much of the model's disparity is explained by the features, and how much is unexplained? In our case, we include race as a feature — so some disparity comes from the model directly using race. But even if we removed race, occupation, education, and hours worked are all correlated with race and would still produce disparate outcomes. The SHAP analysis (next step) will decompose this precisely.

6.2 What Comes Next

The natural next steps for this project are:

  1. SHAP analysis: Decompose the model's predictions feature by feature. Which features drive the racial disparity? Is it occupation? Education? Hours worked? This tells you whether the disparity is "explained" by legitimate factors or not.
  2. Fairness interventions: Apply three strategies — pre-processing (reweighting training data), in-processing (adversarial debiasing), and post-processing (group-specific threshold optimization). For each, plot the Pareto frontier of accuracy vs. equalized odds violation.
  3. Threshold optimization (post-processing): Given XGBoost's well-calibrated scores, the cleanest intervention is to set different classification thresholds \(\tau_0, \tau_1\) for each group to equalize TPR. The optimal thresholds can be found analytically using the ROC curves, and the resulting accuracy loss is the "price of fairness."
  4. HMDA application: Repeat this entire analysis on the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act data with actual loan approval decisions. The stakes are real — approval decisions directly affect wealth accumulation — and the regulatory context (Equal Credit Opportunity Act, Fair Housing Act) makes the fairness definitions legally significant, not just academic.
The Big Picture

What makes this research interesting — and genuinely hard — is that the impossibility theorem tells us there is no purely technical solution. Choosing which fairness criterion to optimize is a policy decision about values: do you prioritize equal treatment (demographic parity), equal errors (equalized odds), or equal meaning of scores (calibration)? Each choice has victims. Good empirical work doesn't pretend otherwise — it quantifies the trade-offs precisely so that decision-makers can make informed choices.

Research Report  ·  Financial Mathematics & Statistics

Climate Risk, Mortgage Markets,
and Racial Equity

A multi-method empirical investigation of racial disparities in mortgage credit access across flood-exposed housing markets in Florida, combining machine learning fairness audits, causal inference, and natural experiments.

Florida HMDA 2018–2022 ACS 2018 California Hurricane Ian (DR-4673) FEMA NFHL

Abstract

We document three interacting forces in the U.S. mortgage market: algorithmic proxy discrimination, persistent lender racial premiums amplified by flood zone exposure, and acute credit access collapse following a major hurricane. Using ACS microdata and XGBoost with 5-fold cross-validation, we show that removing race as a feature leaves approximately 80% of the White–Black prediction gap intact through occupational, educational, and relationship proxies — consistent with Chouldechova’s (2017) impossibility theorem. Using Florida HMDA data (2018–2022), Double/Debiased Machine Learning estimates a +6.8 pp racial rate-spread premium that widens by +2.7 pp inside FEMA Special Flood Hazard Areas. A difference-in-differences design exploiting Hurricane Ian (September 2022) as a natural experiment finds a 7.5 pp White–Black differential in post-storm rate spreads, rising to a net 24.4 pp effect in direct-landfall counties — driven largely by a near-complete collapse of Black mortgage origination. Together these findings suggest that climate shocks compound pre-existing racial credit disparities through both the price and access channels, complementing the housing-market evidence of Box-Couillard (2024).

1. Introduction

Three stylized facts motivate this project. First, persons of color are more likely than white Americans to live in high flood-risk areas, both because of historical redlining and because housing market discrimination makes safe housing more expensive for minority buyers (Box-Couillard, 2024). Second, African American borrowers pay significantly more than observably similar white borrowers for mortgage credit, a premium that persists after controlling for income, loan-to-value ratio, and debt-to-income ratio. Third, climate change is making extreme weather events more frequent and more severe, and the distributional consequences are concentrated among already-vulnerable populations.

This project asks: do climate risks amplify racial disparities in the mortgage credit market, and if so through what mechanisms? We approach the question from three complementary angles:

  1. Algorithmic fairness — can a machine learning income classifier reproduce racial disparities even without an explicit race feature?
  2. Causal estimation — what is the race effect on mortgage rate spreads after partialling out confounders, and does it vary by flood zone?
  3. Natural experiment — does an acute climate shock (Hurricane Ian) differentially restrict Black credit access in affected counties?
Summary of findings
Across all three approaches the evidence converges: racial disparities in the mortgage market are large, robust to controls, amplified inside flood zones, and dramatically worsened by a direct hurricane hit. The dominant mechanism in the post-shock setting is not higher prices for Black borrowers but a collapse in origination volume — the Black mortgage market in Tier-1 Ian counties effectively froze.

2. Data

2.1 ACS Microdata — Income Classification

The fairness audit uses the American Community Survey (ACS) 2018 1-Year person-level file for California, accessed via the folktables library (Ding et al., 2021). The task is binary income classification: predict whether annual income exceeds $50,000. The feature set mirrors variables historically used in automated underwriting: age, class of worker, education, marital status, occupation, place of birth, relationship to household head, usual hours worked, sex, and race.

Why base-rate differences matter
The Chouldechova (2017) impossibility theorem shows that when two groups have different base rates, no classifier can simultaneously satisfy demographic parity, equalized odds, and calibration. The 16 pp base-rate gap between White and Black Californians here guarantees that any well-calibrated model will exhibit apparent unfairness on at least one other fairness metric.

2.2 Florida HMDA Mortgage Data

The causal analysis uses Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA) public loan-level data for Florida, years 2018–2022. HMDA requires lenders to report applicant race/ethnicity, loan amount, purpose, property type, action taken, and the rate spread (APR minus the average prime offer rate, reported for higher-priced loans). After filtering to first-lien conventional single-family purchase mortgages with reported rate spreads, we retain approximately 959,978 loans across 133 Florida counties.

2.3 FEMA National Flood Hazard Layer

Flood zone status is assigned at the census-tract level using FEMA’s National Flood Hazard Layer (NFHL). Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs) are 100-year flood zones (1% annual probability) where federally-backed mortgages require flood insurance. The NFHL is the same dataset used by Box-Couillard (2024) and Hino & Burke (2021).

2.4 Hurricane Ian Treatment Assignment

Hurricane Ian made landfall near Fort Myers Beach, Lee County, on September 28, 2022, as a Category 4 storm (150 mph sustained winds). FEMA declared disaster DR-4673. Treatment intensity is assigned by county:

3. Proxy Discrimination Analysis

3.1 Model and Cross-Validation

We train an XGBoost classifier (gradient-boosted decision trees) on the ACS income task using 5-fold stratified cross-validation. Each fold trains on 80% of the data with an inner 10% sub-split used exclusively for early stopping (patience = 20 rounds), keeping the outer test fold completely untouched during training. Class imbalance is corrected by setting scale_pos_weight = neg / pos, calibrating the predicted probabilities toward the true base rates.

Why 5-fold CV changes the inference
Fairness metrics from a single train/test split are point estimates susceptible to sampling noise. 5-fold CV yields mean ± standard deviation across folds, turning the proxy discrimination finding into a robust statistical claim. The “80% of the gap persists via proxies” result holds consistently across all folds.

3.2 SHAP Decomposition of the Prediction Gap

We compute SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values on each fold’s test set for White and Black applicants separately. The gap contribution of feature $k$ is:

$$\Delta_k = \mathbb{E}[\phi_k \mid \text{White}] - \mathbb{E}[\phi_k \mid \text{Black}]$$

where $\phi_k$ is the SHAP value for feature $k$. By the efficiency property of SHAP, $\sum_k \phi_k = f(x) - \mathbb{E}[f]$, so the sum of all $\Delta_k$ equals the total prediction gap.

3.3 The Chouldechova Impossibility Theorem

Theorem (Chouldechova, 2017)

For a binary classifier $\hat{Y}$ and two groups $A$, $B$ with different base rates $\pi_A \neq \pi_B$, it is impossible to simultaneously satisfy all three of:

  1. Demographic parity: $P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A) = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid B)$
  2. Equalized odds: $\text{TPR}_A = \text{TPR}_B$ and $\text{FPR}_A = \text{FPR}_B$
  3. Calibration: $P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=\hat{p}, A) = P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=\hat{p}, B) = \hat{p}$

Proof sketch: Under calibration, expected approval rates equal base rates ($\hat{\pi}_A = \pi_A$), so $\hat{\pi}_A \neq \hat{\pi}_B$, violating demographic parity. Enforcing DP requires miscalibrating at least one group, which distorts TPR/FPR parity. The system is over-determined with no feasible solution when $\pi_A \neq \pi_B$.

Our model achieves AUC ~0.91 and is well-calibrated. This means the White–Black gap in predicted probabilities faithfully reflects the true income gap. Removing race reduces the gap by only ~20% because XGBoost reconstructs the signal through occupation, education, relationship status, and place of birth — features that encode historical structural disadvantage. This is textbook proxy discrimination.

4. Causal Inference Methods

4.1 Why Regression Alone Is Insufficient

Even OLS with income, LTV, and DTI controls leaves unobserved confounders: neighborhood quality, loan officer discretion, appraisal patterns. We use two approaches designed to address these more rigorously.

4.2 Double / Debiased Machine Learning (DML)

Following Chernozhukov et al. (2018), we partial out confounders using XGBoost with 5-fold cross-fitting:

$$\tilde{Y}_i = Y_i - \hat{\ell}(X_i), \qquad \tilde{D}_i = D_i - \hat{m}(X_i)$$ $$\hat{\theta} = \frac{\sum_i \tilde{D}_i \tilde{Y}_i}{\sum_i \tilde{D}_i^2}$$

where $Y_i$ is rate spread, $D_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{Black}]$, and $X_i$ includes income, LTV, DTI, loan amount, property type, lender fixed effects, and year. Nuisance functions $\hat{\ell}$, $\hat{m}$ are estimated on held-out folds. The Neyman-orthogonal score ensures $\hat{\theta}$ is $\sqrt{n}$-consistent and asymptotically normal even when XGBoost converges at slower non-parametric rates.

4.3 Hurricane Ian DiD — Natural Experiment

Hurricane Ian’s atmospheric path makes county-level treatment assignment plausibly exogenous. We estimate a Two-Way Fixed Effects model with a triple interaction:

$$\text{spread}_{ict} = \alpha_c + \gamma_t + \beta_1 \,\text{Treat}_{c} \cdot \text{Post}_t + \beta_2 \,\text{Black}_{i} + \beta_3 \,\text{Black}_{i} \cdot \text{Treat}_{c} \cdot \text{Post}_t + X_{ict}'\delta + \varepsilon_{ict}$$

where $\alpha_c$ are county fixed effects, $\gamma_t$ year fixed effects, $\text{Post}_t = \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2022]$, and $X_{ict}$ includes income, LTV, and DTI. Standard errors are HC1 sandwich-robust. $\beta_3$ identifies the differential effect of Ian on Black vs. White rate spreads.

Parallel Trends Validation

Pre-period year-by-year treated-minus-control gaps (2018–2021) range 2–3 pp with no systematic drift, supporting the parallel trends assumption.

6.2 Two Channels of Climate-Amplified Racial Disparity

Channel 1 — Flood zone price channel (DML). Under normal market conditions, lenders price Black applicants’ flood risk more aggressively than White applicants’. The +2.7 pp SFHA amplification likely reflects differential property appraisals in minority neighborhoods, stricter LTV requirements, or statistical discrimination based on the correlation between minority borrower location and flood exposure.

Channel 2 — Shock-induced access collapse (Ian DiD). When a major climate shock hits, the Black mortgage market in directly affected areas effectively shuts down. The selection effect is 7× more intense for Black applicants than White applicants in Tier-1 counties. This is an access failure, not a price failure.

Unified story
Minorities are disproportionately located in flood-risk areas partly because housing discrimination makes safe areas more expensive (Box-Couillard, 2024). Once in flood-risk areas, they pay more for mortgage credit under normal conditions (DML result). When a storm hits, their credit access collapses while White borrowers absorb a physical risk premium and continue transacting. Climate change therefore compounds racial wealth inequality through both the price channel and the access channel simultaneously.

6.3 Separating Income from Race

The DML design explicitly controls for income, LTV, DTI, and loan amount. The residualized race coefficient captures rate spread variation orthogonal to all included controls. However, the SHAP analysis shows that income itself is partially endogenous to race through occupational and educational proxies. Truly partialling out race-independent income would require removing proxy channels — which, per the impossibility theorem, introduces other fairness violations. This theoretical bind implies that the DML estimate should be interpreted as a lower bound on total racial discrimination in mortgage pricing.

7. Connection to the Literature

7.1 Box-Couillard (2024) — The Housing Purchase Market Complement

Box-Couillard (2024) studies the same phenomenon from the purchase price side using 26 million repeat-sales transactions (Zillow ZTRAX, 2000–2020, 35 states). Black and Hispanic homebuyers pay 3.3–3.6% more than white buyers outside flood zones, but this premium falls by 60% to ~1% inside flood zones. His mechanism: competition for safe housing is intense outside flood zones, and discrimination is most tolerable to sellers where markets are least contested.

Reconciling opposite flood zone effects
The finding that flood zones reduce the housing purchase premium but increase the mortgage rate premium is not contradictory. A Black buyer in a flood zone may find it easier to get into the housing market (less seller discrimination → lower price) but more expensive to finance the purchase (more lender discrimination → higher rate spread). The two markets are working against minority buyers simultaneously through different mechanisms.

7.2 Hino & Burke (2021)

Using FEMA flood map updates as an instrument, Hino & Burke find a 2.1% average property value discount for newly zoned-in properties. Our FEMA FIRM DiD was designed as a credit-market analog. Our 2.7 pp DML flood-zone amplification is consistent with this magnitude.

7.3 Chouldechova (2017)

The impossibility theorem grounds our proxy discrimination finding theoretically. Any policymaker seeking to fix an automated underwriting system must choose which fairness criterion to prioritize — there is no free lunch when base rates differ across racial groups.

7.4 Chernozhukov et al. (2018)

DML provides Neyman-orthogonal inference: the score for $\theta$ is insensitive to first-order perturbations in the nuisance functions, so XGBoost estimation error in $\hat{\ell}$ and $\hat{m}$ does not bias $\hat{\theta}$. This makes valid inference possible without parametric restrictions on the control function.

The highest-priority methodological extension is the Callaway–Sant’Anna (2021) estimator, which produces clean group-time average treatment effects and removes the “forbidden comparisons” problem in staggered DiD. The highest-priority data extension is merging First Street Foundation property-level flood risk scores to enable a continuous flood risk dose-response curve for the racial mortgage premium.

NASA EONET — Data Overview

Earth Observatory Natural Event Tracker  ·  Free public API, no key required  ·  eonet.gsfc.nasa.gov

What Is EONET?

EONET is NASA's catalog of natural events on Earth — wildfires, storms, earthquakes, floods, and more. It doesn't collect data itself; instead it aggregates reports from 33 external sources (government agencies, research institutions, and disaster-monitoring organizations) into a single searchable API.

Each event in EONET is one named occurrence: a specific wildfire, a specific hurricane, a specific earthquake. As the event evolves, new location updates are added to it. An event stays open while ongoing and becomes closed once it ends.

Data Granularity

Location

Every event has coordinates. Most are a single point (longitude, latitude). Large area events — icebergs, algae blooms — use polygons tracing the boundary.

Time

Each location update carries a timestamp. Most are day-level (midnight UTC as placeholder). Automated sources like IRWIN (wildfires) and USGS (earthquakes) provide hour/minute precision.

Depth over time

Records go back to roughly 2000 for some categories, though coverage improves dramatically after 2016 as more sources were integrated. Earlier years should be treated with caution.

Update frequency

Wildfires and active storms can receive multiple updates per day. Flood and drought events may be reported days or weeks after the fact, from news sources rather than live monitoring.

Reporting Artifacts — What to Watch For

Because EONET aggregates from many sources that were integrated at different times, apparent "trends" in event frequency can be caused by a new data source being added — not by a real change in the world. Key examples found in this analysis:

  • Wildfires (2024): Events jumped from ~97/year to ~5,600/year — almost certainly a reporting expansion by IRWIN, not a real 57× increase in fires. Analysis is capped at 2023.
  • Floods (pre-2025): A 6-year gap in reporting (2019–2024) followed by a sudden surge when GDACS (an automated alert system) was integrated in May 2025. The pre- and post-integration series are not comparable and cannot be joined.
Rule of thumb: before fitting any model, plot the raw monthly event counts and look for sudden step changes — these almost always signal a source change, not a climate signal.

What This Project Models

The analysis fits an inhomogeneous Poisson process to monthly event counts in each category. The model estimates:

  • A seasonal pattern — which months are systematically busier
  • A long-run trend — whether event frequency is increasing or decreasing over time

Two statistical tests check whether any apparent trend is real: the Mann-Kendall test (non-parametric, rank-based) and the GLM trend coefficient (parametric). Model performance is evaluated using expanding-window time series cross-validation — always training on the past and testing on the future.

Key finding: Severe Storms is the only category that is genuinely stationary — both tests agree there is no trend (p = 0.57 / 0.30). Wildfires and Volcanoes show a GLM-detected trend, but the non-parametric test disagrees, and the CV shows the trend hurts out-of-sample predictions — consistent with reporting artifacts rather than real signals.
